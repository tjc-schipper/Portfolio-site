"Getting Hands On"

One thing that was sorely lacking with the introduction of motion controllers in VR was natural physical interaction. This experiment set out to change that.

In theory motion controllers should make interacting with the virtual world effortless and natural, but in practice there are many cases where the illusion breaks down or things just don't work as you'd expect. This framework was created over the course of several days as an exploration of direct object manipulation in virtual reality. The goal was to come up with a flexible and extendable system that feels right

Core idea

"In VR, your hands and your influence on the world are not the same thing."

This idea underpins the design of Hands On. Don't just visualize your hands, visualize what you are doing with them. By making your influence on the virtual world a distinct and visible thing, we can solve many of the problems inherent to 'simple' VR interaction.

Aims

Hands-on set out to accompish four things that would improve user experience significantly.

It is not that these systems did not exist yet, but that I felt they had not been unified and made in a way that feels good to the user

  • 01 Two-handed natural interaction

    We are two-handed creatures. Using two hands is what comes most natural to us. Unfortunately many, if not most, VR experiences handicap you into only using one hand at a time.

  • 02 Physicality

    Everyday objects are not weightless and people do not have infinite strength. For interactions to feel natural, a user needs to feel the weight and volume of an object.

  • 02 Clear feedback

    Because you cannot physically feel a virtual object, extra care must be taken to clearly communicate what the user is doing with it.

Features and demo

Two-handed interaction

The system switches seamlessly between single handed and two handed grabs, allowing the user to adjust any object they are holding just like they would in real life. No longer do you need to strain your wrists to pick up an object just the right way.

Two-handed interaction
Massive objects

Objects respond differently to manipulation depending on their mass. Heavy objects will react slower to input, lagging behind the user's "action" or continuing on their path when the user has already stopped dragging them. Put too much strain on your grabbing hand and the object will slip between your fingers.

This is where the distinction between your hands and your action becomes important. By visualising this difference, the system retains a responsive feeling.

Precision

Not all object manipulation is free and unconstrained. Controls like levers, sliders, switches, dials and others all require the manipulated object to follow a certain path or stay in a certain orientation. Precision placement of objects becomes much easier by applying simple alignment snaps.

Hands On's constraint system can be used and extended for a range of applications. This makes it just as suitable for inserting a puzzle piece or controlling a valve as it is for throwing a ball.

Support for tools

Sometimes, interaction with an object is more elaborate than a simple pick up and drop. Hands On features various grab modes to facilitate different kinds of object usage.

Besides localized and aligned grabs, objects can be configured as a tool which snaps them to a certain orientation when you pick them up and keeps them there until you drop them again. This frees up buttons and allows for tools like screwdrivers, guns, flashlights or even bows.
